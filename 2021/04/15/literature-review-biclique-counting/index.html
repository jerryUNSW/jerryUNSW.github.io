Topic: Biclique Counting

Posted on 2021-04-15

There are many works on k-clique counting. A very trendy idea is to take advantage of an observation in extremal graph theory, the Turan theorem. It states that if the edge density (# of edges/# of vertices) exceeds 1-1/(k-1), then this graph must contain a k-clique. Basically, when the graph is dense enough, we can ensure the existence of a k-clique (a complete graph with k vertices). A recent workt exploits this theorem and uses sampling to estimate the numebr of k-cliques in a given graph.

On bipartite graphs, bicliques are the most cohesive structures. Naturally, we want to ask the following question: if we fixe the size of a biclique, how do we efficiently count its number of occurences? For the ease of discussion, we call the biclique with alpha vertices in upper layer and beta vertices in the lower layer

The most naive way is the list all ab-bicliques in the graph and count the number. A better counting algorithm can be devised by considering biclique cover BC that covers all the vertices. If each ab-biclique is countained in exactly on such biclique, then the total count is the sum of the ab-bicliques in each B’ in BC.

Motivated by recent works on k-clique counting, we are eager to see if we could also exploit some observations from extremal graph theory. Specifically, we would like to know what is the maximum number of edges in a bipartite graph that is free of ab-biclique. It turns out that this is and unsolved problem in mathematics known as the Zarankiewicz problem. While it is unclear exactly how dense a bipartite graph has to be to guarantee the existence of a ab-biclique, the Kővári–Sós–Turán theorem has presented a lower bound. We aim to explore sampling-based method to retireve the biclique-rich subgraphs and estimate the count in these subgraphs. It is also interesting to consider parallel speed up and dynamic graphs.
